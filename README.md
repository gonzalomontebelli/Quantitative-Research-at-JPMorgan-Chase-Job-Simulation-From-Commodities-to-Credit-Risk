## Completed Tasks

### Task 1 — Natural Gas Price Extrapolation Model

**Area:** Commodities Desk · Pricing & Risk

**Problem:** The Commodities desk needed a function capable of estimating the price of natural gas on *any given date* — both for interpolating intra-month historical values and for projecting prices up to one year into the future. The available data consists of monthly historical records.

**My Solution:**

Rather than using a simple linear interpolation (which breaks down outside the data range), I designed a linear regression model with **Harmonic Features**. The continuous function I fitted takes the form:

```
Price(t) = β₀ + β₁·t + β₂·sin(2πt) + β₃·cos(2πt)
```

Where `t` is elapsed time in years from a base date. The `sin` and `cos` terms precisely capture the **annual seasonal cycle** of natural gas (demand peaks in winter for heating, troughs in spring/autumn), while `β₁·t` captures the **long-term price trend**.

**Outcome:** A callable function `estimate_price(date_str)` that accepts any date string and returns the estimated USD price, valid for any time horizon.

### Task 2 — Natural Gas Storage Contract Pricer

**Area:** Commodities Desk · Structured Products

**Problem:** Building on the pricing model from Task 1, the desk needed a function to value natural gas storage contracts. These contracts involve multiple injection and withdrawal dates, physical facility constraints, and several distinct cost components.

**My Solution:**

I developed the `price_storage_contract()` function, which models the contract as a **chronological cash flow simulation**. The function:

1. **Builds a unified timeline** of all events (injections and withdrawals) sorted chronologically
2. **Enforces physical constraints** at each event:
   - Maximum storage capacity (`max_volume`)
   - Maximum flow rate per event (`max_rate`)
   - Cannot withdraw more gas than currently stored
3. **Calculates cash flows** for each operation, including variable injection/withdrawal costs
4. **Computes fixed monthly storage costs** over the full contract period, with correct handling of partial months (standard industry practice)

The resulting contract value is the **Fair Value** the bank would use as the basis for client pricing.

**Validated Test Case:**
```
Injection:    Jun–Jul 2024  →  1M MMBtu @ $2.00–$2.10/MMBtu
Withdrawal:   Dec 2024–Jan 2025  →  1M MMBtu @ $3.50–$3.80/MMBtu
Storage cost: $50,000/month
→ Contract Fair Value: $1,250,000
```

**Stack:** `pandas` · `python-dateutil`

### Task 3 — Credit Risk Model & Expected Loss Calculator

**Area:** Retail Banking · Credit Risk Management

**Problem:** The Retail Credit team needed a model to predict whether a borrower would default, and to translate that probability into a concrete **Expected Loss (EL)** figure under the Basel II/III regulatory framework.

**My Solution:**

I implemented a full Machine Learning pipeline in two stages:

**Stage 1 — Predictive Model Selection:**

I compared two approaches on the loan dataset:

| Model | AUC Score |
|-------|-----------|
| Random Forest (100 trees, max_depth=5) | ~1.00 |
| Logistic Regression + StandardScaler | ~1.00 |

Both models achieved a perfect AUC on the test set, indicating highly linear relationships between features and the target variable. I selected **Logistic Regression** for its superior **institutional interpretability** and ease of regulatory auditability.

**Stage 2 — Expected Loss Calculation:**

The `calculate_expected_loss()` function implements the Basel formula:

```
EL = PD × EAD × LGD
```

- **PD (Probability of Default):** Estimated by the Logistic Regression model
- **EAD (Exposure at Default):** The outstanding loan amount
- **LGD (Loss Given Default):** Fixed at 90% (10% Recovery Rate)

**Features used:** `credit_lines_outstanding`, `loan_amt_outstanding`, `total_debt_outstanding`, `income`, `years_employed`, `fico_score`

**Stack:** `pandas` · `numpy` · `scikit-learn`

### Task 4 — Optimal FICO Score Bucketing (Dynamic Programming)

**Area:** Retail Banking · Credit Scoring & Rating Systems

**Problem:** Credit rating systems require assigning a **discrete rating (1–10)** to each borrower, but the optimal boundaries between categories are not obvious. Where exactly does "Rating 7" end and "Rating 6" begin? Arbitrary cutoffs waste statistical information.

**My Solution:**

I implemented a **Dynamic Programming (DP)** algorithm that finds the optimal bucket boundaries across 10 rating categories by maximizing the total **Log-Likelihood** of the model. The intuition: the best boundaries are those that create groups where borrowers are most homogeneous within each bucket (same true PD) and most heterogeneous across buckets.

**The algorithm in 3 steps:**

1. **Prefix Sum precomputation:** I precompute cumulative sums of defaults `k` and records `n` for each unique FICO score, enabling O(1) range queries over any score interval.

2. **Dynamic Programming:** `dp[b][i]` stores the maximum log-likelihood achievable by segmenting the first `i` unique FICO scores into `b` buckets. The recurrence is:

```
dp[b][i] = max over j { dp[b-1][j] + LL(j, i-1) }
```

3. **Backtracking:** I reconstruct the optimal boundaries by tracing back through the decision table.

**Sample output — FICO to Rating map:**

| Rating | FICO Range | Estimated PD |
|--------|------------|--------------|
| 10 (Best) | 740–850 | < 5% |
| ... | ... | ... |
| 1 (Worst) | 550–599 | > 30% |

**Complexity:** O(N² × B) where N = number of unique FICO scores, B = number of buckets.

**Stack:** `pandas` · `numpy`
